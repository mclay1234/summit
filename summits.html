There are three types of summits in the Alberta Rockies:

  1. Official: The government named them and they appear on official maps. Relatively few peaks are officially named and official status is not correlated with the greatness of the mountain, or even whether the bump of land with the name is an actual summit. Syncline Mountain and Pinetop Hill are great examples.
  2. Unofficial, but in published books: The author has climbed an unnamed peak, usually one that already has an unofficial but common name, and published the route and name in a guidebook. The peak then seems official in some capacity. Smutwood Peak is a good example.
  3. Unofficial, but on websites: Someone who has a website has climbed a peak and given it name. Others follow and the name spreads and appears on random mapping websites (if worthy, the peak may end up in a published guidebook). These ones are usually absurd, yet spread like wildfire in the age of the internet. Ben's Hill is a good example. Someone placed a geocache on the side of Prairie Mountain in 2007, called it "Child of Prairie Mountain", but then found the name "Ben" spelled out with rocks and decided it should be Ben's Hill. Now this point halfway up the mountain, named after transient graffiti and with a prominence of perhaps 20 m, is considered a summit by the internet. I call it a summit in my list below too, but really, it's not.
All this does of course mean my summit list is absurdly arbitrary, so take the summit count with a significant grain of salt. I haven't counted many that seem to have official internet status, and I haven't held back in making up my own summits when I felt like it. I suppose my simple definition of a summit is that if I purposely set out to reach a high point and reached it, then it's a summit, but I doubt I strictly follow that rule. I'm just out there for fun; I don't hike up peaks because the government, books, or websites tell me to, and my summit list and other stats serve only to justify silly celebrations like gondola rides or cupcakes.

In the summit list below I've tried to identify the source of the summit. Official ones were easy, obviously. For unofficial ones, published books were given priority over websites, even though many published peaks were first described on websites. For unofficial ones not in books, I tried to find the original reference that started its rise to internet summit status, but I've very likely misassigned many of these. It's also very important to note that in some cases the presence of a trip in a book or on a website does not imply that the author meant for it to achieve internet summit status; that designation is being made by those with lists like mine below.
